Clinical History

A lesion on the parietal scalp of a 28-year-old female. Reason for consultation. In a few areas, the lesion shows features which suggest to me that it may represent a cellular blue nevus. However, I am concerned about the possibility that it may represent a malignant blue nevus or malignant melanoma.

Introduction:

This case is presented as it was originally received and reported, several years ago … Clinical information. A lesion on the parietal scalp of a 28-year-old female. Reason for consultation. In a few areas, the lesion shows features which suggest to me that it may represent a cellular blue nevus. However, I am concerned about the possibility that it may represent a malignant blue nevus or malignant melanoma.

Pathological/Microscopic Findings and any Immunohistochemical or Other Studies:

This material is unusually difficult. The sections show a bulky melanocytic tumor, located in the dermis and the subcutaneous tissue. The tumor presents, in some areas, an organoid appearance, with nests and fascicles of spindle- shaped cells and, in some areas, with dendritic pigmented cells. These attributes are quite characteristic of cellular blue nevi. In other areas, however, there are broad sheets of less differentiated epithelioid melanocytes, showing significant though not striking nuclear atypia, with prominent nuclear line, and with easily detectable mitoses. In addition, there are a few abnormal mitoses. Although, as you point out, the lesion is reasonably well-circumscribed, in some areas it appears to have an infiltrative relationship with surrounding collagen and adipose tissue.

There is no necrosis.

Although I would place this lesion, for purposes of categorization, in the cellular blue nevus category, I cannot regard it as a benign lesion. The important features in reaching this conclusion are the extensive epithelioid cell areas, the moderate nuclear atypia, the occasional mitoses, the presence of abnormal mitoses, and the focally infiltrative pattern (with a couple of satellites) at the periphery. On the other hand, the lesion lacks necrosis, one of the most widely cited diagnostic criteria for so called "malignant blue nevus". Thus, for diagnostic and therapeutic purposes, I would sign this is lesion out descriptively, as "dermal spindle and epithelioid cell melanocytic tumor, probably of low malignant potential".

Differential Diagnoses:

Atypical Cellular Blue Nevus vs. Malignant Blue Nevus.

Final Diagnosis:

Malignant Blue Nevus.

Case Discussion:

Diagnosis: Skin, parietal scalp: Dermal spindle and epithelioid cell melanocytic tumor, probably of low malignant potential

Comment. Because of the organoid differentiation, I believe that this lesion is certainly not a metastatic melanoma. Furthermore, I do not believe that it is a characteristic primary malignant melanoma, because of the absence of an in situ component and the presence of background components of cellular and banal blue nevus. It is possible that some consultants might regard this lesion is a benign cellular blue nevus. However, I believe that it may have at least a potential for local recurrence, and possibly some low potential for metastasis. As you know, cellular blue nevi have been associated with metastases to regional lymph nodes, followed by long survival. Thus, I would regard the prognosis for this patient as excellent, provided that local control is achieved, probably with a modest local reexcision. I do not believe that a formal wide excision as typically advocated for high-risk primary cutaneous melanoma is indicated.

Three Years Later. Thank you for your letter with follow-up information and a slide from the above captioned case. I have reviewed the previous material in some detail. The slide shows a bulky cutaneous tumor, with attributes of a cellular blue nevus, namely the bottom heavy and somewhat "dumbbell" configuration, the presence of nests of clear spindle cells embedded in a background of eosinophilic cuboidal cells, and the presence of associated dendritic heavily pigmented cells in the reticular dermis reminiscent of common blue nevi. There are no areas of necrosis, there is no high grade nuclear atypia, and mitotic figures although readily observed and not frequent. I counted approximately 2-3 mitotic figures per square millimeter in various areas of the tumor.

Review of the Literature/Treatment Options:

Malignant blue nevus is a rare diagnosis, and only a few substantial case series have been published over the last several decades [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]. These lesions need to be distinguished from common blue nevi, from cellular blue nevi and atypical cellular blue nevi, and from melanoma. Common blue nevi are generally small lesions that lack all of the features of malignant blue nevi as discussed below. The first use of the term “cellular blue nevus” to distinguish these lesions from melanoma is attributed to Allen in 1949 [7]. As recently reviewed by Zembowicz [8], these lesions can occur at all ages and common locations include the sacrococcygeal region and buttock, followed by scalp, face and extremities, and a wide range of less common locations. Clinically these lesions are firm bluish black to bluish gray dome shaped nodules. Most are in the 1 to 2 cm range, but a few may be much larger. Histologically these lesions most commonly exhibit a “mixed-biphasic” pattern, with pigmented spindle cell fascicles alternating with clear epithelioid cells arranged in nests; the latter may be characterized as an “alveolar” pattern. There may be areas of a more uniform spindle cell pattern, which has been termed “fascicular and neuronevoid” [1]. There is no overlying junctional component. There is typically a background banal blue nevus component. A common pattern is that of a “dumbbell” where two separate masses in the dermis and subcutis are joined by a narrow isthmus. Cytologically, the nuclei tend to be relatively pale with regular nuclear membranes, finally stippled chromatin, and inconspicuous nucleoli. There is no severe uniform cytologic atypia or necrosis. Mitotic figures are usually absent and in any case should be sparse (e.g. less than one per square millimeter, and only a few per lesion).

Atypical cellular blue nevus has been described in a few publications as a variant of cellular blue nevus. Characteristics that have been used in establishing these diagnoses have included larger size, ulceration, cytologic atypia, and increased mitotic activity. In a recent study of nine cases, criteria used for the diagnosis include architectural atypia (infiltrative margin and/or asymmetry) and/or cytologic atypia (hypercellularity, nuclear pleomorphism, hyperchromasia, mitotic figures, and/or necrosis) [3]. These features overlap with those of malignant blue nevi and with malignant melanomas in general, and in lesser degree may also be seen in benign cellular blue nevi. Not unexpectedly, there is lack of consensus among experts in the use of this diagnosis [9]. The behavior of these lesions is generally benign. Local recurrences may occur, and lymph node “metastases” may also be present in regional nodes [1]. There is debate as to whether these represent nodal nevi or true metastases [10]. In any event, distant metastasis and death have been rarely if ever been reported in lesions definitively characterized as atypical cellular blue nevi. Nevertheless, there is overlap among cases with this diagnosis, and those diagnosed as malignant blue nevi [4].

Malignant blue nevi were also first described by Allen. These tumors resembled blue nevi and were associated with distant metastasis and a high rate of mortality. To establish a diagnosis of malignant blue nevus, it is probably necessary to see evidence of a remnant of cellular blue nevus and/or common blue nevus. It has been suggested that these lesions should be most appropriately termed “blue nevus-like melanoma” [6]. Although it is reasonable to regard a malignant blue nevus as a form of melanoma, the requirement for evidence of the background lesion would seem to set these lesions apart from other melanomas. In addition, blue nevi and related malignancies tend to have activating mutations in proteins that signal through G- protein coupled receptors and are important in early melanoblast proliferation in the dermis (i.e. GnaQ and Gna11). These lesions are less likely to exhibit mutations of the genes involved in the pathogenesis of common nevi or melanomas, such as BRAF, and NRAS or c-Kit [11].

The diagnosis of a malignant blue nevus, given the existence of the background blue nevus or cellular blue nevus, is based on three major attributes of malignancy, including first, the presence of focal areas of severe uniform cytologic atypia characterized by nuclear hyperchromatism, irregularity and pleomorphism, often with prominent nucleoli, second, the presence of increased mitotic activity usually including atypical mitotic figures, and third, by the existence of necrosis and/or ulceration. Any of these attributes may be absent or present in slight degree in cases with proven malignant outcomes. This gives rise to uncertainty between the differential diagnoses of atypical cellular blue nevus and malignant blue nevus. In some of these circumstances, a classification of the lesion as a “melanocytic tumor of uncertain malignant potential” is the most prudent course to follow. In such a case, a clear description of the differential diagnostic possibilities should be given, in order that therapy can be planned appropriately [10, 12, 13].

This present case has clearly behaved as a malignant blue nevus, having a background of a banal blue nevus and cellular blue nevus-like areas. The clinical course has been clearly malignant with an initial presentation with lymph node metastases followed by death from disease within two years in this young patient. In a recent study of 26 patients with “malignant blue nevus”, the prognosis was dismal and was similar to usual forms of melanoma of similar microstage. Management for these lesions was recommended to be planned according to appropriate melanoma management guidelines [6].

To characterize this lesion as a cellular blue nevus would clearly have been a significant under-call and might well have led to under-treatment as cellular blue nevi, according to some clinical texts, are wholly benign lesions which do not require reexcision if margins are positive or close. The literature for atypical cellular blue nevi might also give this impression. The more recent literature recognizes the existence of uncertainty in the identification of malignancy in atypical melanocytic lesions of a number of subsets including the cellular vs. atypical vs. malignant blue nevus class of diseases. Although a benign diagnosis was initially favored in this case, a recommendation was given for complete local excision, and recurrence at the local site was not observed in follow-up. It is a mistake in my opinion to render a diagnosis (or a prognosis) that is more definitive than is warranted by the evidence. Patients and clinicians given information as to the range of possible diagnoses can plan therapeutic and life decisions more appropriately than if they are given either false confidence or an inappropriately negative prognosis at the time of initial diagnosis in these difficult cases.

Conclusion(s):

Overall comment:

The outcome of this case confirms that lesions with morphology of cellular blue nevi but with mitotic activity must be treated with some circumspection. However, I do not believe that these should be termed malignant melanomas uncritically. Now that this lesion has metastasized systemically of course, it must unfortunately qualify as a malignant blue nevus. As with any malignant melanocytic tumor, the attributes of the primary lesion are not likely to have much influence on the course of the disease now that it has established systemic metastases.

As events transpired, this lesion resulted in the death of this young patient within two years.

This lesion did not recur locally following complete excision and therefore must have metastasized systemically prior to its complete removal. This outcome was difficult to predict from histology of the primary tumor, however as a general "rule of thumb" any melanocytic lesion (with the exception of a few well-defined lesion such as Spitz tumors with a low mitotic rate) characterized by a combination of cytologic atypia and dermal mitotic activity should be treated with circumspection, and the possibility of metastatic potential cannot be ruled out. The prognosis, accordingly, should be somewhat guarded in these lesions and they should be regarded as being of uncertain malignant potential.
